## Biography

There is a particular kind of intelligence that cannot be taught in a classroom — one built at the intersection of strategy and consequence, between a financial model on a screen and a business that lives or dies by its cash flow. Edmond Moepswa has spent his career developing that intelligence deliberately, across disciplines, until the ability to connect them became his primary skill.

He is, at his core, a systems thinker. Not the kind whose work lives in abstractions, but the kind who has stood in a café kitchen managing P&L statements before the morning rush, structured insurance portfolios for multi-million-pula construction projects, and designed wealth plans for clients whose legacies span generations. Every chapter of his career added a layer to the same structure: a working theory of how organisations, families, and economies actually function — and how to build them to last.

His professional formation began in Canberra, Australia, where he studied Finance and Financial Planning at the University of Canberra, later completing a Graduate Diploma in Business Management. While still a student, he placed 4th nationally in the AMP University Challenge — a competitive financial planning and investment management competition held at the national level — a result that reflected both the rigour of his programme and an early aptitude for applied analysis under pressure. His early career in the Australian hospitality sector — progressing from all-rounder to Head Barista to General Manager across four venues — gave him ground-level understanding of the pressure small business owners carry: managing staff, reading P&L statements in real time, optimising cash flow with limited room for error. It was during this period, as General Manager of Morning Dew, that he personally designed and built the café's website from scratch — an early, self-initiated expression of the same instinct that now drives BridgeArc. That experience never left him. It became the lens through which he later assessed every financial plan and every client system.

The transition into wealth management was natural for someone already thinking in systems. At Bravium, Fiducian Financial Services, and Profile Financial Services in Sydney, he worked within the Australian financial planning framework — advising on asset allocation, risk, and intergenerational wealth structures. The work required rigour, long-horizon thinking, and comfort with complexity.

In 2024, Edmond returned to Botswana and took on the role of Risk & Insurance Manager at HSNV Group, one of the country's leading construction project management and property development firms. The role was operationally demanding: managing insurance portfolios spanning Contractors All Risk, Professional Indemnity, Public Liability, and Motor Fleet; building a corporate employee benefits program from scratch; maintaining regulatory compliance; and leading due diligence for land and investment property acquisitions. In 2025, he stepped back from full-time employment; in 2026, he committed to BridgeArc full-time.

During his time at HSNV Group, Edmond was given an unusual direct assignment by the MD: take on operational oversight of Tonota FC, a First Division North football club that HSNV had acquired. He began attending matches, setting up on-pitch branding with HSNV-branded materials, and assessing operations across players, staff, management, and the fanbase. He reported back to the MD with actionable recommendations. Tonota FC was promoted to the Botswana Premier League in March 2026, an outcome Edmond contributed to before leaving the group in 2025.

Today, Edmond operates through **BridgeArc** — a digital services practice built on the premise that technology, deployed with genuine business understanding, is one of the most powerful levers available to any organisation. BridgeArc works across the full spectrum: established businesses looking to modernise their operations, enterprises requiring robust digital infrastructure, government and institutional clients with complex delivery requirements, and international clients across platforms like Upwork and Contra. The commitment to emerging businesses and founders runs deep, but the practice is built to serve any organisation where operational capability is the constraint and where the work can be done properly.

Every system Edmond builds reflects his background: designed to last, built around how your business actually works, and documented for handover when the engagement ends.

### A Note on Platform Availability

Not every platform operates in every country or accepts every merchant. The following reflects the current situation for Botswana-based businesses and should be confirmed at scoping, as availability can change.

**Payment Gateways:**

- **Stripe** — Not available to Botswana-registered businesses. Stripe does not support merchant accounts in Botswana as of 2025. It cannot be used for local BWP settlement.
- **DPO PayGate (Network)** — The primary established local gateway. Works with FNB Botswana and Stanbic Bank. Supports Visa, Mastercard, Amex, Online EFT, and mobile money. Merchant account required; setup involves bank approval. PCI DSS Level 1 compliant.
- **Orange Money Web Payments** — Available to registered Orange Money merchants in Botswana. Excellent for reaching unbanked and mobile-first customers.
- **PayPal** — Available for international customer payments. High transaction fees; useful for international buyer trust. Does not settle in BWP.
- **Dodo Payments** — Operates as Merchant of Record; handles international tax and compliance. Suitable for digital products, SaaS, and subscriptions sold internationally. Supports 150+ countries, 80+ currencies. Not a local BWP gateway.
- **Paddle** — Also a Merchant of Record platform; suited to SaaS and digital subscriptions. Not a local BWP gateway.
- **Gumroad** — For digital products and creator content; simple setup, international reach. Note: Gumroad requires at least one active commercial product listing in your store — it is not designed as a pure delivery layer. BridgeArc's implementation pairs each free resource with a planned paid product in the same Gumroad store, satisfying this requirement from the outset. Not a local BWP gateway.

_All payment gateway integrations require the client to have an active merchant account with the provider. Approval timelines and criteria vary by bank and provider and are outside BridgeArc's control._

**WhatsApp Integration:**

- **WhatsApp Business API (Meta)** — Requires Meta Business verification (business registration documents, Meta Business Manager account). Approval typically takes 1–7 business days but can be rejected for unverified business details, non-compliant use cases, or inconsistencies between submitted documents and Meta records. Approval is not guaranteed and is entirely at Meta's discretion. Costs are conversation-based (marketing, utility, authentication, and service categories priced differently).
- **WhatsApp Click-to-Chat** — No API, no approval, no cost. A simple link (wa.me/[number]?text=[pre-filled message]) opens WhatsApp with a pre-populated message. Included by default in all BridgeArc website builds at no extra cost. Suitable for most small businesses in Botswana.
- The WhatsApp API integration add-on is built assuming the client can obtain or already holds Meta Business verification. If the client is rejected, the delivered integration cannot be activated.

**SMS Gateways:**

- **Twilio** — Supports Botswana (Mascom, Orange, beMobile carriers). Outbound SMS only; two-way SMS is not supported in Botswana through Twilio. Alphanumeric sender IDs are used. Per-message cost approximately $0.10 USD to Botswana numbers; client bears this cost.
- **AWS SNS (Simple Notification Service)** — Alternative to Twilio for outbound SMS. Similar coverage and cost structure. Client bears per-message cost.
- _Note: Short code SMS (dedicated inbound numbers) is not currently available in Botswana through major international SMS providers._

**Communication & Collaboration Platforms:**

- **Slack** — Available globally, no approval required; client creates their own workspace.
- **Telegram** — Available globally; bot and channel integration is straightforward.
- Availability of any other platform should be verified per-project.

### 6. SEO, GEO & Google Business

**Who it's for:** Businesses that have a website — or are about to launch one — and want to be found. Traditional search rankings, AI-generated answers, and local map visibility are three separate but connected surfaces. This service addresses all three, in a framework that is coherent with the rest of the BridgeArc stack.

A note on what this is not: SEO is a long-term compounding activity. Results are measured in months, not days. This service is structured to build the right foundations and operate them consistently — not to promise rankings on a timeline.

---

#### What the three components mean in practice

**Search Engine Optimisation (SEO)** is the technical and content discipline that makes your site visible in Google's traditional link results. It covers the structural signals Google uses to decide whether your pages are credible, relevant, and worth indexing — and what queries they should rank for. Technical SEO (site architecture, page speed, crawlability, schema markup) is addressed at build time on every BridgeArc web project. Ongoing SEO extends this into content, link signals, and continuous performance monitoring.

**Generative Engine Optimisation (GEO)** is the emerging discipline of getting your business cited in AI-generated answers — in Google AI Overviews, ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini. When someone asks an AI tool "who is the best [service] in [city]?" the answer comes from structured content, entity consistency, and authoritative signals — not from keyword density. GEO is SEO's next layer, not its replacement. It requires content structured for direct answers (not just for clicks), consistent entity information across the web, and sufficient authority signals for AI retrieval systems to trust and cite your content. AI-referred web sessions grew over 500% year-on-year in the first half of 2025; this channel is now material for most businesses.

**Google Business Profile (GBP) optimisation** is the most immediate lever for any business serving customers in a specific geography. A complete, actively managed GBP listing drives map visibility, appears in Google AI Overviews for local queries, and is one of the highest-ROI actions available to any Botswana-market SME. It is also the foundation of GEO for local businesses — AI tools cite GBP data when answering local discovery queries.

### Delivery Timelines — How They Work

Standard timelines are measured in working business days from **project kickoff** — defined as the point at which the deposit is received and the client brief is confirmed. Client review time does not count against the timeline clock: if a design is delivered on Day 3 and the client takes 4 days to review it, the clock resumes from Day 3 when feedback arrives, not Day 7.

**Example: Foundation Build (single page) — standard 5–8 business days**

| Day               | What Happens                                                                |
| ----------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Day 0 — Monday    | Deposit received and brief confirmed. Clock starts.                         |
| Day 1–2 — Tue/Wed | Visual design: layout, typography, colour, and brand direction              |
| Day 3 — Thursday  | First design mockup delivered to client for review                          |
| —                 | Client reviews and sends feedback _(client time — clock paused)_            |
| Day 4 — Friday    | Feedback received. Revisions begin.                                         |
| Day 5 — Monday    | Second design iteration delivered. Client provides sign-off _(client time)_ |
| Day 6–7 — Tue/Wed | Development: responsiveness, SEO setup, form wiring, integrations           |
| Day 7–8 — Wed/Thu | Testing, deployment to Vercel, DNS configuration, browser checks            |
| Day 8 — Thursday  | Site live. Handover documentation sent. Project complete.                   |

This example assumes one round of feedback and reasonably prompt client responses. Projects with slower client review cycles or more complex change requests will extend accordingly — and that extension is not counted as a delivery delay.

**Priority delivery (+20%):** The same project targeted at approximately 4–6 business days. BridgeArc moves it to the front of the active queue; some rescheduling of other work is required but manageable.

**Rush delivery (+35%):** The same project targeted at 3–4 business days or fewer. This requires compressing development into extended hours and displacing existing scheduled commitments. Rush requests are accepted only when calendar availability permits and are confirmed after a scoping call. The surcharge reflects the operational cost of rescheduling, not a markup on the work itself.

## Guiding Philosophy

**Build things that hold.**

Most of what Edmond has learned — across hospitality, financial planning, corporate risk, and now software — points at the same principle: systems built only for the best case don't survive contact with reality. You find this out in a café kitchen at 7am when three staff don't show. You find it in a financial plan that assumed smooth markets. You find it in a codebase that works perfectly until the client's first real customer tries to do something unexpected. The lesson, every time, is the same: design for pressure, not just performance.

This isn't pessimism. It's the most optimistic thing you can do for someone — build them something that will still be working a year after you've handed it over.

**The gap is usually infrastructure, not intelligence.**

There's a particular frustration in watching a capable business underperform because its tools are the wrong size — either too expensive and complex to implement, or too simple to handle actual growth. That gap is real, and it's not about the quality of the people running the business. It's an access problem. The same conviction underpins BridgeArc: the principles and tools that make large organisations effective are learnable and buildable at any scale. That started as a theory and became a practice.

**Understand the whole before touching any part.**

There's a constant pull in technology toward solutions that arrive before the problem is clear. A new tool gets proposed before anyone has mapped what the current process actually is. A website gets built before anyone has established what it's supposed to make people do. Edmond's instinct — built across enough roles to see the pattern — runs in the opposite direction: slow down before scoping, map the whole system before recommending any change, and don't touch a part until you understand how it connects to everything else. Solutions without that groundwork are expensive guesses.

**The job isn't done at launch.**

A delivered system that the client can't maintain, explain, or adapt isn't actually done — it's just handed off. The measure of a build is whether it still runs cleanly six months later, managed by the people who work inside the business, without needing to come back and ask how it works. Documentation, training, handover — these aren't administrative tasks bolted on at the end. They're part of the work from the beginning.
