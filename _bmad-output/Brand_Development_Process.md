**Service component of web design & development.** Brand development is delivered as part of a web engagement — not sold separately. The scope, timeline, and deliverables depend on which service tier the client is buying.

**Important distinction:** This is the brand development process for **Edmond Moepswa's personal website** as an individual professional. It is not the brand process for BridgeArc (the business entity). BridgeArc has its own separate brand identity. This document covers how Edmond's personal brand is developed and deployed as part of his personal website build.

## Phase 1 — Intake & Discovery

**Duration:** 30–60 minutes (discovery call)
**When:** Before any billable work. Every engagement begins here.

### 1.1 — Classify the Engagement

At the discovery call, determine which service tier the client needs:

**Decision tree:**

- Does the client have existing brand assets (logo, colours, fonts)? If yes, and they just want a website → **Foundation Build** (Launch, Presence, or Growth depending on page count)
- Does the client need a brand built from scratch AND a website? → **Growth Build** (minimum)
- Is this a rebrand of an existing business? → Assess whether existing equity should be preserved (refresh) or reconsidered (rebrand). Rebrands require at least Growth build scope.

### 1.2 — Discovery Call Agenda (30 minutes)

1. **What does the business do?** (One sentence. No jargon.)
2. **Who do they serve?** (Specific audience — not "everyone")
3. **What problem does it solve?** (What competitors don't solve, or solve differently)
4. **What's the budget?** (Match to service tier)
5. **What's the timeline?** (Is there a launch date?)
6. **Do they have existing brand assets?** (Logo, colours, fonts — or starting from zero?)
7. **Where will the brand appear?** (Website only, or also social media, business cards, signage?)
8. **Are there colours they refuse to use?** (And colours they're drawn to?)
9. **Name 3 brands they admire and why?** (Outside their industry — this reveals taste, not competitive pressure)
10. **Who makes the final decision?** (One person or committee?)

### 1.3 — Competitive Landscape Quick-Check

For every engagement, audit 3–5 direct competitors before any visual work:

- Logo type (wordmark / symbol / monogram / combination / emblem)
- Primary colour
- Typography style (serif / sans-serif / display)
- Visual language (photography / illustration / minimal / maximalist)
- Tagline or strapline
- Price signal (premium / accessible / value-positioned)
- Differentiation claim

**Purpose:** Not to find inspiration. To identify where differentiation is available. A brand that positions itself in the same visual quadrant as three competitors has not differentiated.

### 1.4 — Cultural & Market Context

For Botswana-based clients, verify:

- Local colour associations (what communicates "premium" in the Botswana market?)
- Language requirements (does the brand need to operate in Setswana or English?)
- Trust signals (what makes a digital presence feel credible to this specific audience?)
- Payment gateway availability (DPO PayGate, Orange Money, PayPal, Dodo — which applies to this client's customer base?)

## Phase 2 — Brand Strategy

**Duration:** 1–2 days
**When:** For Growth builds. Optional for Presence builds.

### 2.1 — Brand Brief (1 page)

A written brief is produced after the discovery call. It is the first deliverable of any brand engagement. It must definitively answer:

1. **Purpose** — Why does this brand exist beyond generating revenue?
2. **Audience** — Who is this brand for? Defined with precision. Name a specific person.
3. **Problem** — What problem does this brand solve that competitors do not solve?
4. **Values** — Three to five non-negotiable principles. Must already be true, not aspirational.
5. **Success** — What does success look like in 12 months? Measurable signals.
6. **Exclusions** — What must this brand never be associated with?
7. **Competitive landscape** — Three admired brands outside the category. Three inside. Why?
8. **Primary communication** — If the brand could only communicate one thing, what is it?
9. **Decision-making** — Who are the final sign-off stakeholders?
10. **Constraints** — Legal, technical, cultural, or budgetary constraints.

**Brief anti-patterns to reject:**

- "We want something clean and modern" — Probe for specifics. Clean relative to what?
- "Make it stand out" — Standing out is a consequence of strategic clarity, not a design instruction.
- "Similar to [competitor] but different" — The brief must resolve the tension, not preserve it.
- A brief written after the design has already been started — Start over.

### 2.2 — Positioning Statement

The positioning statement is an internal working document, not a tagline:

> For [target audience] who [specific need or problem], [brand name] is the [category definition] that [primary differentiating benefit] because [reason to believe].

**Quality test:** If it takes more than one sentence to explain, it is not finished. Strip it back.

### 2.3 — Brand Architecture

For solo practitioners and small agencies, monolithic architecture is almost always correct: one brand, one mark, one name. Sub-brands come later, when the portfolio genuinely justifies the complexity.

### 2.4 — Brand Archetypes (for Growth builds)

Identify the primary archetype (dominant character governing 70–80% of brand decisions) and a secondary archetype (supporting character). Use as a creative filter throughout the visual and verbal identity work.

| Archetype     | Tone                                   | Example Sectors                         |
| ------------- | -------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| **Sage**      | Authoritative, analytical, trustworthy | Consulting, legal, financial services   |
| **Creator**   | Imaginative, inventive, perfectionist  | Design, art, artisan, creative services |
| **Ruler**     | Authoritative, commanding, premium     | Enterprise, institutional, luxury       |
| **Explorer**  | Independent, adventurous, pioneering   | Tourism, outdoor, adventure             |
| **Everyman**  | Friendly, unpretentious, authentic     | Food & beverage, community, retail      |
| **Caregiver** | Warm, supportive, patient              | Healthcare, NGO, education              |
| **Hero**      | Confident, bold, inspiring             | Fitness, performance, competition       |

## Phase 3 — Visual Identity Direction

**Duration:** 2–3 days
**Gate:** Visual direction approved before any logo work begins.

### 3.1 — Creative Brief for Visual Development

Before any visual exploration begins, translate the brand brief into a specific creative brief:

| Section                     | Content                                                                     |
| --------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Brand essence**           | Single word or short phrase distilling the brand's core identity            |
| **Strategic idea**          | Central concept the visual identity must communicate                        |
| **Personality descriptors** | Five specific adjectives, ranked in order of priority                       |
| **Personality opposites**   | Five adjectives describing what this brand must never feel like             |
| **Audience context**        | Who will encounter this identity, on what surfaces, in what emotional state |
| **Competitor context**      | What the visual landscape looks like; where differentiation is available    |
| **Primary applications**    | Top three to five touchpoints the identity must work across                 |
| **Technical constraints**   | Screen-only or print required; minimum size requirements; multi-language    |
| **Success criteria**        | How will we know the identity is working?                                   |

### 3.2 — Logo Type Selection

Select the logo type on strategic grounds, not aesthetic preference:

| Type                      | When to use                                               | Default for personal sites                           |
| ------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| **Wordmark**              | Brands where the name itself is the primary asset         | Default for professional services, consulting, legal |
| **Lettermark**            | Brands with longer names building institutional authority | Rare — only for well-established names               |
| **Combination mark**      | New brands needing flexibility across touchpoints         | Default for most marketing sites                     |
| **Pictorial/Iconic mark** | Brands with a clear visual metaphor                       | Default for artisan, food, events, beauty            |
| **Emblem**                | Institutions, heritage brands, certifications             | For church/NGO and institutional clients             |

### 3.3 — Sketching Phase (Paper First)

Spend 20–30% of total logo design time sketching on paper. The goal is volume: 50–100 rough thumbnails across multiple conceptual territories, not five polished concepts.

**Rules:**

- Every idea gets a sketch, including the obvious and the absurd
- Work at thumbnail scale (no larger than a postage stamp) to force simplicity
- Step away for at least 4 hours before reviewing

**Conceptual territory audit — ensure coverage of:**

- Literal/descriptive concept (does the logo communicate what the business does?)
- Abstract/geometric concept
- Typographic concept (wordmark)
- Negative space usage
- Structural simplicity (one shape, one idea)

### 3.4 — Vector Development (Figma or Illustrator)

Build selected concepts in vector software:

- Use whole-number dimensions
- Build from primitive geometric shapes (circles, squares, rectangles, triangles)
- Name every layer and group
- Align everything to the pixel grid
- Delete stray anchor points regularly

### 3.5 — Construction Grid Systems

Apply the construction grid after the initial form is built:

- **Base Grid:** All proportions expressible as whole-number multiples of a unit
- **Construction Grid:** Verify structural integrity — curves should be clean circular arcs, angles should be intentional (30°, 45°, 60°, 90°)
- **Lockup Grid:** Define spatial relationship between mark and wordmark
- **Clearspace Grid:** Define protected area (minimum clearspace = 1X on all sides, where X = cap height of wordmark or height of primary mark)

### 3.6 — Optical Correction

Geometric perfection is not visual perfection:

- Circles must be ~5% larger than equivalent squares to appear equal
- Horizontal strokes appear heavier than vertical — fractionally thin horizontals
- Optically centred elements sit slightly above mathematical centre
- Rotate the mark 180° — if balance feels different, negative space is asymmetric

### 3.7 — At-Size Quality Testing

- Does the mark read at 32×32px? At 16×16px (favicon)?
- Does the wordmark track correctly at every size?
- Are all strokes visually consistent at 100% zoom?
- Does the mark feel balanced at arm's length?

### 3.8 — Logo System Completion

Deliver the complete system, not a single mark:

| Variant                     | Use                                                            |
| --------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Primary lockup — horizontal | Primary use in most digital and print contexts                 |
| Primary lockup — stacked    | Square-constrained contexts                                    |
| Mark only                   | Social avatars, favicons, embossed materials                   |
| Wordmark only               | Contexts where mark is too small or crowded                    |
| Full colour                 | Primary colour treatment                                       |
| Reversed                    | White on dark backgrounds and dark photography                 |
| Monochrome black            | Photocopies, single-colour print                               |
| Monochrome white            | Embossing on light materials                                   |
| Minimum size                | Defined separately in pixels (digital) and millimetres (print) |
| Clearspace                  | Annotated diagram                                              |
| Prohibited uses             | 6–10 specific incorrect applications illustrated               |

## Phase 4 — Colour System

**Duration:** 1–2 days
**Gate:** Colour system approved.

### 4.1 — Colour Selection

Colours are selected based on the brand brief, not aesthetic preference.

**Default colour system for most builds:**

| Layer                     | Colours                                          | Purpose                            |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------- |
| **Primary (1–2 colours)** | Main brand colour(s) — carry the identity signal | Logo, headings, primary CTAs       |
| **Neutral (4–6 colours)** | Near-black, mid-grey, light grey, off-white      | Backgrounds, body text, borders    |
| **Accent (1 colour)**     | High-contrast action colour                      | Buttons, links, interactive states |
| **Semantic**              | Success, warning, error, info                    | UI state communication             |

**Colour associations by sector (from research):**

| Sector                     | Recommended primary                             | Recommended accent                                | Avoid                      |
| -------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------- |
| **SaaS / Tech**            | Near-black (`#09090B`) or deep navy (`#0F172A`) | Electric blue (`#2563EB`) or teal                 | Purple (overused)          |
| **Financial Services**     | Deep navy or charcoal                           | Gold or warm amber                                | Orange (low-trust signal)  |
| **Professional Services**  | Charcoal or slate                               | Forest green or deep burgundy                     | Neon colours               |
| **Artisan / Craft**        | Warm earth tones (terracotta, burnt sienna)     | Warm amber or sage                                | Electric/neon colours      |
| **Food & Hospitality**     | Warm neutrals (cream, warm grey)                | Amber or terracotta                               | Cold blues                 |
| **Beauty & Wellness**      | Soft neutrals (ivory, warm white)               | Muted rose or sage                                | Harsh primaries            |
| **Events & Entertainment** | Deep near-black                                 | Luminous single-colour accent                     | Multiple competing accents |
| **NGO / Church**           | Warm neutrals                                   | Single warm accent                                | Cold corporate palettes    |
| **E-commerce**             | Near-black or deep navy                         | Single strong accent matching product photography | Multiple competing colours |

### 4.2 — Colour Palette Architecture

Every complete brand colour system is built in four layers:

1. **Primary palette** (2–3 colours max): Carry the brand's primary identity signal
2. **Secondary palette** (2–4 colours): Supporting colours for data viz, categorisation, seasonal campaigns
3. **Neutral palette** (4–6 colours): The ground on which everything else sits. Most visible layer. Most overlooked.
4. **Semantic colours**: Error, success, warning, information — derived from or harmonious with the primary palette

### 4.3 — Colour Technical Documentation

Every colour documented in all required formats:

| Format          | Use                                    |
| --------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| **HEX**         | Digital: web, apps, design tools       |
| **RGB**         | Digital: screen-based presentation     |
| **CMYK**        | Offset and digital print               |
| **Pantone PMS** | Premium printing, merchandise, signage |

**WCAG Accessibility (non-negotiable):**

| Level             | Minimum contrast ratio | Applies to                              |
| ----------------- | ---------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| **AA**            | 4.5:1                  | Normal text                             |
| **AA large text** | 3:1                    | Large text (18pt+ regular, 14pt+ bold)  |
| **AAA**           | 7:1                    | Enhanced readability (preferred target) |
| **UI components** | 3:1                    | Icons, form borders, focus indicators   |

Tools: `contrast.tools`, WebAIM Contrast Checker, Figma Accessibility Plugin.

### 4.4 — The 60-30-10 Ratio

In any layout:

- **60%** — Dominant neutral (white, off-white, light grey, or dark background in dark mode)
- **30%** — Primary brand colour applied with intention
- **10%** — Accent colour at highest contrast

## Phase 5 — Typography System

**Duration:** 1–2 days
**Gate:** Typography system approved.

### 5.1 — Typeface Selection

**Default typefaces for builds:**

| Use Case                                       | Heading Font                                              | Body Font                      |
| ---------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| **Marketing sites (Launch, Presence, Growth)** | Inter or Geist                                            | Inter or Geist                 |
| **Professional services**                      | Inter (headings) + Source Serif or Freight Text (display) | Inter                          |
| **SaaS / Tech**                                | Geist or Inter                                            | Inter or Geist Mono (for code) |
| **Luxury / Premium**                           | A serif display font (via Adobe Fonts)                    | Humanist sans-serif            |
| **Artisan builds**                             | A characterful display or serif                           | Clean sans-serif (Inter)       |
| **NGO/Church**                                 | Humanist sans-serif (warm, accessible)                    | Same sans-serif                |

**Selection criteria:**

1. **Personality fit** — Does the typeface carry the right associations?
2. **Legibility at body sizes** — Is it comfortable at 14–16px on screen? Test it.
3. **Character range** — Does it include all characters for the primary language(s)?
4. **Display quality** — At headline sizes (48px+), does it have the optical quality required?
5. **Technical performance** — File size, variable font support, self-hosting permitted?

### 5.2 — Type Scale

Derive every size from a single base size (16px) and a ratio:

| Level | Name     | Size    | Weight          | Line Height | Letter Spacing     | Use                       |
| ----- | -------- | ------- | --------------- | ----------- | ------------------ | ------------------------- |
| T1    | Display  | 64–96px | Light/Regular   | 1.0–1.1     | -0.02em to -0.04em | Hero headings             |
| T2    | H1       | 48–64px | Regular/Medium  | 1.1–1.2     | -0.02em            | Page heading              |
| T3    | H2       | 32–48px | Regular/Medium  | 1.2         | -0.01em            | Section heading           |
| T4    | H3       | 24–32px | Medium/SemiBold | 1.25–1.3    | 0                  | Sub-section heading       |
| T5    | H4       | 18–24px | SemiBold        | 1.3         | 0                  | Minor heading, card title |
| T6    | Lead     | 18–20px | Regular         | 1.5–1.6     | 0                  | Intro paragraphs          |
| T7    | Body     | 15–17px | Regular         | 1.5–1.7     | 0                  | Primary body text         |
| T8    | Small    | 12–14px | Regular/Medium  | 1.4–1.5     | +0.01em            | Captions, labels          |
| T9    | UI Label | 11–13px | Medium/SemiBold | 1.2–1.4     | +0.02em to +0.06em | Navigation, tags          |

**Optical correction at display sizes:** At sizes above 32px, headline text almost always requires manual letter-spacing adjustment. Test visually, adjust, document the corrected value.

### 5.3 — Web Typography Implementation

**Self-hosted variable fonts preferred.** Served from the site's own infrastructure (Vercel) — no third-party dependency.

```css
@font-face {
  font-family: 'Brand Typeface';
  src: url('/fonts/BrandTypeface-VF.woff2') format('woff2');
  font-weight: 100 900;
  font-display: swap;
}
```

**Font performance budget:** Total web font loading weight should not exceed 100KB per typeface (woff2, variable, subsetted).

**Rules never violated:**

- Never use all-caps for extended body text
- Never set body text below 14px on mobile
- Never use justified text on screen without hyphenation
- Never set more than 75 characters per line in body text. Optimal: 55–65
- Always define a complete typographic hierarchy before beginning layout work

## Phase 6 — Supporting Visual Language

**Duration:** 2–3 days
**When:** Growth builds.

### 6.1 — Iconography System

**Decisions made before any icons are drawn:**

| Decision             | Default                                                                      |
| -------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Style**            | Outline (Lucide or Heroicons) — matches the visual weight of most logo marks |
| **Corner treatment** | Sharp (0px) for tech/professional; slightly rounded (2–4px) for warmth       |
| **Stroke weight**    | 2px (Regular) — matches Lucide defaults                                      |
| **Grid**             | 24×24px canonical grid                                                       |
| **Library**          | Lucide (MIT license, open source, consistent family)                         |

**Quality standards:**

- Every icon optically balanced, not just geometrically centred
- All anchor points on whole-number pixel coordinates at 24×24
- All icons at the same size have the same perceived visual weight
- Test every icon at 16×16 — if elements disappear, simplify

### 6.2 — Photography Art Direction

**Photography brief for each client:**

| Element              | Decision                                                   |
| -------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Subjects**         | People / product / architecture / environment              |
| **Environment**      | Studio / on-location (specific location types)             |
| **Lighting**         | Natural / artificial; hard / soft                          |
| **Colour treatment** | Neutral / warm grade / cool grade / high contrast          |
| **Composition**      | Tight crop / wide environment; centred / asymmetric        |
| **Mood**             | Aspirational / documentary / intimate / commanding / quiet |
| **Post-processing**  | Minimal editing / branded colour grade / grain             |
| **Prohibited**       | Stock photography clichés specific to the client's sector  |

**Stock photography clichés to eliminate (by sector):**

- **Professional services:** Diverse team meeting in glass-walled office; person on laptop smiling inexplicably; two people shaking hands
- **Technology:** Floating holographic data; binary code on dark background; glowing circuit boards
- **Fintech:** Gold coins; piggy banks; currency symbols; rising chart lines on a gradient
- **Real estate:** Anonymous exterior shot; couple standing outside a property
- **Healthcare:** Stethoscope on white background; gloved hands; doctor looking at a tablet
- **Food:** Generic plated dish on white background with no context

**Portfolio builds:** The portfolio is the commercial centrepiece. Client must supply high-resolution images and sample video links at project kickoff. The visual quality of the delivered site depends directly on the quality of assets provided. Embedded video via YouTube or Vimeo. Direct video hosting on Vercel is not recommended for large files — Cloudflare R2 + Stream is the upgrade path.

### 6.3 — Pattern & Texture System

Derived from the logo geometry or the brand's product/process. Applied as:

- Backgrounds
- Borders or dividers
- Campaign material
- Merchandise (if applicable)

### 6.4 — Motion Language (for Growth builds)

**Motion personality based on brand archetype:**

| Archetype     | Motion Character                   | Duration  | Easing                         |
| ------------- | ---------------------------------- | --------- | ------------------------------ |
| **Sage**      | Deterministic, tight, no overshoot | 100–250ms | Ease-out                       |
| **Creator**   | Spring physics, slight overshoot   | 200–400ms | Spring easing                  |
| **Ruler**     | Deliberate, unhurried entrance     | 300–600ms | Custom spring, low damping     |
| **Explorer**  | Dynamic, directional               | 200–350ms | Ease-out with slight overshoot |
| **Everyman**  | Slightly elastic, soft             | 200–350ms | Spring with medium damping     |
| **Caregiver** | Gentle, warm                       | 250–400ms | Ease-out, soft deceleration    |
| **Hero**      | Bold, fast, responsive             | 150–250ms | Ease-out, no overshoot         |

**Core animation patterns:**

- **Entrance:** Elements enter with ease-out (never ease-in), translate 8–24px, fade in simultaneously, stagger 30–80ms between elements
- **Page transitions:** Crossfade (simple), slide (communicates depth), or wipe (brand presence)
- **Hover:** Translate 2–4px, subtle shadow or scale increase, under 80ms delay
- **Scroll-driven:** Reveal on enter, parallax depth (sparingly), pinned sections, progress-linked transformation

**Performance rules:**

- Animate only `transform` and `opacity` — GPU-composited, no layout recalculation
- Never animate `width`, `height`, `top`, `left`, `margin`, `padding` directly
- Test on a mid-range Android device with throttled CPU

**Accessibility:** Always respect `prefers-reduced-motion`. Complete non-animated fallback for every animated element.

## Phase 7 — Design System & Component Architecture

**Duration:** 3–5 days
**When:** Growth builds.

### 7.1 — Design Token Architecture

Three-tier token system mapped to Tailwind CSS configuration:

**Tier 1 — Primitive tokens (raw values):**

```css
--color-primary: #18181b;
--color-accent: #2563eb;
--color-surface: #fafafa;
--space-1: 4px;
--space-2: 8px;
--space-4: 16px;
```

**Tier 2 — Semantic tokens (purpose-named):**

```css
--color-text-primary: var(--color-primary);
--color-text-secondary: #71717a;
--color-surface-default: var(--color-surface);
--color-border: #e4e4e7;
```

**Tier 3 — Component tokens (component-specific):**

```css
--button-primary-bg: var(--color-accent);
--button-primary-text: #ffffff;
--button-border-radius: 6px;
```

### 7.2 — Spacing System

8px base grid. Nothing in the design system uses a spacing value outside this scale:

| Token       | Value | Use                                     |
| ----------- | ----- | --------------------------------------- |
| `space-0.5` | 2px   | Micro spacing: icon-text gap            |
| `space-1`   | 4px   | Tight internal spacing                  |
| `space-2`   | 8px   | Standard component internal spacing     |
| `space-3`   | 12px  | Comfortable component internal spacing  |
| `space-4`   | 16px  | Standard component-to-component spacing |
| `space-6`   | 24px  | Comfortable element separation          |
| `space-8`   | 32px  | Section sub-element spacing             |
| `space-12`  | 48px  | Major section spacing                   |
| `space-16`  | 64px  | Large section vertical rhythm           |
| `space-24`  | 96px  | Full-page section separation            |

### 7.3 — Component Library (Figma)

Built in atomic hierarchy:

1. **Tokens** — Colours, sizes, spacing, shadows
2. **Atoms** — Typography styles, buttons, form inputs, badges, icons, avatars, dividers
3. **Molecules** — Form groups, search bars, navigation items, card headers, metric displays
4. **Organisms** — Navigation header, hero sections, feature sections, pricing tables, testimonial sections, footer
5. **Templates** — Page-level structures without real content
6. **Pages** — Templates populated with actual content

**Construction standards:**

- Every component uses Auto Layout — never manually positioned
- Every component has variants for every documented state
- Components reference colour and text styles from the style library — never hardcoded values
- Naming convention: `ComponentCategory/ComponentName/Variant`

### 7.4 — CMS Selection

**Keystatic (Git-based CMS)** — Default for:

- Marketing sites (Presence, Growth builds)
- Clients with 1–2 content editors, <5 content types, no relational data
- Zero infrastructure cost — content stored as Markdown/JSON in Git

**Payload CMS (TypeScript-native, self-hosted)** — Default for:

- E-commerce, professional services firms, financial services
- Custom SaaS
- Clients needing custom field logic, role-based access control, relational data
- Free software — infrastructure costs $0 on Vercel Hobby + Supabase free + Cloudflare R2 free

### 7.5 — Database & Auth Selection

**Supabase (PostgreSQL + Auth + Storage + Realtime)** — Default for:

- E-commerce backend
- Client portal with document storage
- Custom SaaS
- Free tier: 500 MB database, 1 GB storage, 2 GB bandwidth/month
- Row Level Security for role-based access control

**Clerk (authentication with pre-built UI)** — Default when:

- Rich auth UI is needed (sign-in, sign-up, user profile components)
- Social login (Google, GitHub, Microsoft) is required
- Multi-tenant organisations needed
- Free tier: 10,000 MAU — covers virtually every SME application
- Alternative to Supabase Auth when richer UI is preferred

**Auth selection decision:**

- Simple auth with database integration → Supabase Auth
- Rich UI components, social login, multi-tenant → Clerk
- Expected to exceed 10,000 MAU → Evaluate Kinde (lighter, cheaper at scale)

### 7.6 — Hosting & Deployment

**Vercel (default hosting)** — All builds deploy to Vercel:

- Hobby tier: 100 GB bandwidth/month, 100 build hours/month, 100K serverless invocations
- Automatic preview deployments for client review (share real URLs, not screenshots)
- Edge functions for geolocation and personalisation (e.g., BWP vs USD pricing)
- Built-in cron jobs for sitemap regeneration and scheduled tasks
- Deploy to Frankfurt (fra1) for best latency to Botswana clients

**Cloudflare Pages (alternative)** — For image-heavy sites approaching Vercel bandwidth limits:

- Unlimited bandwidth, unlimited requests
- Cloudflare R2 for asset storage (10 GB free, no egress fees)

### 7.7 — Design System in Practice

The design system is built in Figma as the single source of truth. Tailwind CSS configuration is derived from the design tokens. Every page design references the system — no independently created styles or components.

**Deliverables:**

- Figma design system file (foundations, atoms, molecules, organisms, templates)
- Tailwind CSS configuration file (`tailwind.config.ts`) with design tokens
- CSS custom properties file for runtime theming (dark mode, brand variants)
- Component documentation with usage examples

## Phase 8 — Verbal Identity

**Duration:** 1–2 days
**When:** Growth builds. Optional for Presence builds.

### 8.1 — Brand Voice

**Voice development:**

1. Write ten sentences only this brand would write
2. Write ten sentences this brand would never say
3. Identify the 3–5 qualities explaining the difference
4. Name and define each quality as a voice pillar

**Voice pillar documentation:**

| Pillar    | We are             | We are not            |
| --------- | ------------------ | --------------------- |
| [Quality] | [Example sentence] | [Contrasting example] |

### 8.2 — Messaging Framework

| Level                   | Definition                                                                           |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Brand promise**       | Single overarching claim to the audience                                             |
| **Value propositions**  | Specific benefits per audience segment (one per segment)                             |
| **Proof points**        | Specific, verifiable evidence — named case studies, quantified outcomes, credentials |
| **Key messages**        | 3–5 core messages repeated across every touchpoint                                   |
| **Supporting messages** | Context and channel-specific messages                                                |

**Proof point quality standard:** Must be specific enough to be verified. "We reduced onboarding from 12 minutes to under 4 minutes" not "We've helped hundreds of clients."

### 8.3 — Copy Standards

- Remove every adverb that modifies a claim rather than specifying it
- Remove every claim of leadership without proof
- Remove "we" from the first sentence of every page — start with the customer's problem
- Test every sentence against "So what?"
- Maximum two claims per section
- Read copy aloud — if it stumbles, it hasn't been edited

## Common Logo Design Failures — Anti-Checklist

**Construction failures:**

- [ ] Gradient as structural element in the primary mark (must work in one flat colour first)
- [ ] Skipping the construction grid (mark that looks fine at 500px but breaks at other scales)
- [ ] Pen-drawn freehand curves introducing optical inconsistencies
- [ ] Anchor points not on whole-number coordinates (anti-aliasing artefacts)

**Conceptual failures:**

- [ ] Over-rationalised geometry (claiming golden ratio when it isn't real)
- [ ] Literal metaphor (construction company with a building, financial brand with a rising chart)
- [ ] Stock icon modified as the mark
- [ ] Combining elements from multiple directions (produces incoherence)

**Typography failures:**

- [ ] Trendy display typefaces in the wordmark (condensed neo-grotesques, ultra-wide geometric sans-serifs — dated within 3–5 years)
- [ ] Multiple typefaces in the same wordmark
- [ ] Unmodified commercial typeface as the wordmark (every other brand using the same font has the same "wordmark")
- [ ] Letterspacing applied uniformly rather than optically

**Complexity failures:**

- [ ] Visual complexity that breaks at small sizes
- [ ] Too many elements asking for attention simultaneously
